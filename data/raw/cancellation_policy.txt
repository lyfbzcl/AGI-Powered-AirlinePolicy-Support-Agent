Cancellation Policy

This cancellation policy explains how passengers may cancel a booking before travel and what consequences may apply after cancellation. Cancellation rules depend on the fare purchased, the route, the time remaining before departure, and whether the cancellation is voluntary or caused by a disruption outside the passenger's control. The specific fare conditions attached to the booking govern the outcome of a cancellation request. Passengers should review those conditions at the time of booking because low promotional fares often carry stricter cancellation penalties than flexible fares.

How to cancel:
Passengers may cancel an eligible booking through the airline website, mobile app, customer support center, airport ticket desk, or the original travel agency that issued the ticket. The cancellation must be completed before the published departure time of the first unused flight on the itinerary unless the fare rules specifically permit a later request. A request started but not fully confirmed before departure may not prevent the booking from being treated as a no-show. Where a reservation includes multiple passengers, the person making the cancellation should ensure the request clearly identifies whether all travelers or only selected travelers are being cancelled.

Cancellation before departure:
If cancellation is made before departure, the ticket may be cancelled with no penalty, with a published cancellation fee, or without any residual value, depending on the fare family. Flexible and fully refundable fares generally allow cancellation with little or no penalty, while basic and discounted fares often carry a fixed fee or complete forfeiture of the base fare. In many cases, taxes or government charges for unused travel may still be refundable even when the fare itself is not. Optional purchases such as paid seats, priority boarding, lounge access, prepaid baggage, and onboard meal selections are usually non-refundable unless the airline is unable to provide the service purchased.

Risk of no-show:
If a passenger does not cancel before the flight departs and also does not board, the booking may be classified as a no-show. A no-show may result in loss of the entire ticket value, automatic cancellation of all remaining flights in the same itinerary, and forfeiture of any onward or return segments. Some fares also impose a separate no-show penalty in addition to standard cancellation fees. Passengers who know they will miss their flight should cancel as soon as possible to preserve any remaining value allowed under the fare rules.

Travel credits after cancellation:
When a cancelled fare is not eligible for cash refund but retains residual value, the airline may issue a travel credit. Travel credits are usually valid for a limited period measured from the original ticket issue date or the original departure date, depending on fare rules. Credits are commonly non-transferable and must be used by the same passenger named on the original ticket. Any future booking made with a credit may still require payment of a fare difference, taxes, or service fee. If the new itinerary is cheaper than the credit value, some fare types preserve the remaining balance while others forfeit the unused amount.

Cooling-off and legally required cancellation rights:
Certain markets may require a short period after booking during which a passenger may cancel without penalty, provided the booking was made sufficiently far in advance of departure. Where such legal rights apply, the airline will follow the applicable law for that market. Outside those legally required circumstances, the airline is not obliged to provide a free cancellation period unless the fare conditions specifically include one. Passengers should not assume that a booking can always be cancelled without penalty simply because the request is made shortly after purchase.

Special circumstances:
Requests to cancel due to serious illness, death of an immediate family member, military orders, jury service, visa refusal, or other exceptional events may be reviewed on a case-by-case basis if supporting documents are provided. Approval of an exception is not automatic and may be limited to a waiver of the cancellation fee, issuance of a travel credit, or refund of only the unused taxes and charges. The airline may require original or certified documents and may refuse documents submitted after an unreasonable delay. Group bookings, charter fares, and tickets issued under corporate or tour contracts may follow separate cancellation terms.

Bookings with partner airlines:
If an itinerary includes flights operated by another airline, cancellation of the overall booking may be restricted by the most restrictive fare rule in the itinerary. Some partner segments cannot be partially cancelled online and may require assistance from customer support or the original issuing agent. Cancelling one sector of a multi-city or return itinerary may cause the remaining fare to be recalculated, which can reduce or eliminate any residual value.

Passenger responsibilities:
Passengers are responsible for verifying that the cancellation confirmation has been issued and for keeping a copy of the confirmation email, reference number, and any travel credit details. If payment was made through a third-party agency, the airline may not be able to confirm how long that agency takes to process the cancellation. Passengers must also ensure they comply with any deadline for cancelling ancillary services, airport hotel packages, or separately booked ground transport.

Important note:
Cancellation and refund are related but not identical. A ticket may be successfully cancelled while still producing only a partial refund, a travel credit, or no remaining value at all. Passengers should review fare rules carefully before cancelling.
