Refund Policy

This refund policy explains when a passenger may receive a refund for an unused or partially used ticket and how refunds are processed. Refund eligibility depends on the fare rules attached to the ticket, the reason the refund is requested, the route traveled, the method of payment, and any local laws that provide additional consumer rights. The ticket conditions displayed at the time of booking remain the primary source for determining whether a refund is permitted. Passengers should review those fare conditions carefully before purchasing, because some fares are intentionally sold at a lower price in exchange for strict refund limits.

Voluntary refunds:
If a passenger chooses not to travel and cancels a booking before the scheduled departure of the first unused flight coupon, a refund may be available if the fare purchased is refundable. Refundable fares may be refunded to the original form of payment after deduction of any applicable cancellation fee, service charge, used portion of the itinerary, or non-refundable taxes and surcharges where permitted. Some fares allow a full refund only when cancellation is completed a specified number of hours or days before departure. If the passenger cancels after that deadline, a reduced refund or a travel credit may apply instead. Voluntary refund requests must be made by the passenger named on the ticket or by the person who paid for the booking if the airline requires payment verification.

Non-refundable tickets:
Most basic, promotional, or deeply discounted fares are non-refundable. When a non-refundable ticket is cancelled before departure, the ticket value may be retained as a travel credit if the fare rules specifically allow credit issuance. Any such credit is usually non-transferable, must be used within the validity period stated on the booking, and may only be applied toward future travel issued in the same currency or country of sale. Service fees, seat selection charges, baggage fees, carbon offset contributions, and other optional purchases are generally non-refundable unless required by law or unless the related service could not be provided by the airline.

Involuntary refunds:
An involuntary refund may be offered when the airline cancels a flight, fails to operate a flight reasonably according to schedule, causes a significant schedule change, or is unable to provide the class of service purchased and the passenger does not accept an offered alternative. In those circumstances, the passenger may usually request a refund for the unused ticket portion. If travel has already begun and the disruption affects the remaining itinerary, the passenger may also request a refund of the unused balance and, where applicable, the difference between the fare paid and the transportation actually provided. Eligibility may depend on whether the passenger accepted rebooking, completed part of the journey voluntarily, or used the ticket after being informed of the schedule change.

Partially used tickets:
When only part of an itinerary has been used, the refund amount is not automatically equal to the unused sector price shown in the booking path. The airline may recalculate the value of the transportation already used based on the fare conditions that applied to the flown sectors and deduct that amount from the total ticket price. If the remaining balance after recalculation is zero, no refund is due. Certain taxes, fees, or government charges associated with unused sectors may still be refundable even where the base fare is not. Refunds of unused airport or passenger service charges may require a separate request.

Refund request process:
Tickets purchased directly from the airline should normally be refunded through the airline's website, call center, ticket office, or customer support channels. Tickets purchased through a travel agency, online travel platform, or other third party usually must be refunded by that original issuing agent unless the airline advises otherwise. The passenger may be asked to provide booking details, contact information, banking confirmation, proof of identity, and documents supporting the reason for the request. Refund claims based on illness, bereavement, duplicate ticketing, or visa refusal may require official evidence and will be reviewed under the relevant fare rules or compassionate policy, if available.

Refund timing:
Approved refunds are typically processed within 7 to 14 business days for credit card purchases and may take longer for cash, bank transfer, voucher, or third-party payment methods. Processing times can also increase during major disruptions or periods of unusually high claim volume. The airline's processing time refers only to the time required to approve and release the refund. Posting times for the passenger's bank, card issuer, or payment provider are outside the airline's control. If a refund is issued in a currency different from the original transaction currency, exchange rate differences and bank charges may reduce the amount received.

Non-refundable amounts and exceptions:
Some taxes, supplier fees, insurance premiums, booking fees, and optional services remain non-refundable even on refundable tickets. A ticket used in violation of fare rules, obtained fraudulently, charged back without basis, or altered after issuance may be denied refund in full or in part. No-show passengers who fail to cancel before departure may lose the right to any refund or credit unless the fare rules state otherwise. When a passenger is denied boarding or prevented from traveling because required travel documents were missing, invalid, or expired, the refund will be handled according to the underlying fare rules and may be limited.

Important note:
Submitting a refund request does not guarantee approval. Passengers should cancel any booking they do not intend to use before departure, keep copies of their confirmation and receipts, and allow sufficient time for review where documents are required.
